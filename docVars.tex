\abstracttext{This thesis focuses on the concept of open source in the development of contemporary electronic music instruments. A brief history of sharing, electronics design and electronic music offers context for an analysis of recent projects, linking open source to previous practices of invention, do it yourself, hacking, bending and self-learning. These projects all revolve around sharing and open source, and are further contextualized by a series of interviews with their authors. By working through results and processes, this document exposes that the current understanding of open source invention is meaningful as the logical continuation of longstanding practices in the field. Those have sought to fine-tune existing technologies to the needs of the artist. Through a personalization of the devices, open source allows individuals to curate more satisfying interactions with instruments, regardless of their own contributions to the community.} % note that abstract has to be on one line; to get a line break, use \\~\\
